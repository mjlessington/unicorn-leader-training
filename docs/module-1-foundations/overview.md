# Module 1: Foundations of Unicorn Leadership

**Duration:** 90 minutes | **Format:** Lecture + Discussion + Self-Assessment

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, participants will be able to:

1. Identify the 12 unicorn habits from Vanderbloemen's framework and explain why they matter in IT leadership
2. Articulate Brene Brown's definition of courageous leadership and its four skill sets
3. Explain why the intersection of these frameworks creates a uniquely powerful model for technology leaders
4. Assess their current leadership profile against both frameworks

## The Leadership Crisis in Technology

Technology leadership is at an inflection point. According to Gartner, 70% of IT leaders cite "people challenges" as their top barrier to digital transformation — not technology, not budget, not strategy. The problem isn't that IT leaders lack technical skills. The problem is that the industry has historically promoted for technical excellence while ignoring the human capabilities that determine whether teams and transformations succeed.

This creates a predictable pattern:

1. A brilliant engineer gets promoted to lead a team
2. They apply engineering thinking to people problems
3. They optimize for efficiency instead of trust
4. Their team delivers but doesn't grow, innovate, or stay
5. The leader burns out trying harder at the wrong things

Both Vanderbloemen and Brown have diagnosed this pattern from different angles, and their solutions are remarkably complementary.

## What Makes a Unicorn?

William Vanderbloemen spent years studying over 30,000 candidates to identify what separates truly exceptional professionals — "unicorns" — from everyone else. His finding: **it's not talent, intelligence, or experience. It's habits.**

The 12 unicorn habits are organized into three categories:

### Habits of Relationship
These are the habits that determine how you connect with and influence others:

- **Drives to the Basket** — Takes initiative without being asked. Doesn't wait for permission or perfect conditions.
- **Genuine Authenticity** — Shows up as the same person in every room. No corporate mask, no performance.
- **Solves Problems Before Anyone Knows They Exist** — Anticipates issues and addresses them proactively.
- **Infectious Energy** — Brings consistent positive energy that lifts the people around them.

### Habits of Mindset
These habits shape how you think about your work and your world:

- **Thinks Abundantly** — Operates from a mindset of abundance rather than scarcity. Believes there is enough success for everyone.
- **Stays Curious** — Never stops learning. Asks questions others don't think to ask.
- **Adapts Quickly** — Embraces change rather than resisting it. Adjusts rapidly when conditions shift.
- **Owns the Outcome** — Takes full responsibility for results, not just effort. Doesn't externalize blame.

### Habits of Execution
These habits determine how you convert intention into impact:

- **Sees Around Corners** — Anticipates trends, threats, and opportunities before they're obvious.
- **Goes the Extra Mile** — Consistently exceeds expectations without being asked or incentivized.
- **Makes Others Feel Special** — Treats every person with genuine care and attention.
- **Builds Bridges** — Creates connections between people, ideas, and organizations that others don't see.

:::tip[Key Insight]
Vanderbloemen's critical finding is that these habits are **learnable**. They are not personality traits or genetic gifts. They are practiced behaviors that anyone can develop with intention and effort.
:::

## What Makes a Daring Leader?

Brene Brown defines a leader as *"anyone who takes responsibility for finding the potential in people and processes, and who has the courage to develop that potential."* By this definition, leadership is not a title — it's a choice.

Her research identifies **four skill sets** of courageous leadership:

### 1. Rumbling with Vulnerability
The willingness to show up when you can't control the outcome. In IT leadership, this means admitting you don't know the answer, acknowledging a project is failing before the metrics force you to, and creating space for your team to take risks.

### 2. Living into Your Values
Identifying your core values (not aspirational ones — the ones you actually live by) and developing the behaviors that operationalize them. Most leaders can name their values; few can describe the specific behaviors that prove they're living them.

### 3. BRAVING Trust
Trust is not a single thing — it's a collection of seven specific behaviors captured in the BRAVING acronym:
- **B**oundaries
- **R**eliability
- **A**ccountability
- **V**ault (keeping confidences)
- **I**ntegrity
- **N**on-judgment
- **G**enerosity

### 4. Learning to Rise
The ability to recover from setbacks, failures, and disappointments. In technology, where failure is frequent and public, this skill set is critical for sustained leadership effectiveness.

## The Intersection: Why IT Leaders Need Both

| Challenge | Vanderbloemen's Answer | Brown's Answer | Combined Approach |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Team doesn't take initiative | Drive to the basket (model it) | Create psychological safety so risk-taking is safe | Model initiative AND remove the fear of failure |
| Lack of trust after reorg | Build bridges, make others feel special | Apply BRAVING framework systematically | Rebuild trust through specific behaviors AND genuine relational investment |
| Innovation stagnation | Stay curious, adapt quickly | Rumble with vulnerability — name the fear | Cultivate curiosity AND create space for the discomfort of not knowing |
| Burnout across the team | Infectious energy, think abundantly | Living your values — name what matters | Sustain energy through meaning AND protect what matters most |

The combined model gives IT leaders both the **what** (specific habits to practice daily) and the **how** (the emotional infrastructure to sustain those habits under pressure).
