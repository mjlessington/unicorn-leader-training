# The 12 Unicorn Traits: Applied to IT Leadership

Each of Vanderbloemen's 12 habits takes on specific meaning in the technology leadership context. This section maps each trait to real-world IT scenarios and connects it to Brown's courage framework.

---

## Habits of Relationship

### 1. Drives to the Basket

**In IT Leadership:** You don't wait for the quarterly review to surface a problem. You don't wait for the vendor to fix the issue. You don't wait for someone to assign you the migration. You see what needs to happen and you move.

**What this looks like:**
- Volunteering to own the incident response before being asked
- Proposing the architecture decision rather than waiting for consensus to emerge
- Reaching out to the business stakeholder directly rather than routing through layers

**Brown's connection:** Driving to the basket requires vulnerability — you might be wrong, you might overstep, you might fail publicly. Leaders who armor up against these risks also armor up against initiative.

:::info[IT Leader Scenario]
Your company's legacy ERP system is creating data quality issues that ripple into three downstream systems. No one owns the problem because it crosses team boundaries. A unicorn IT leader doesn't wait for a directive — they draft a proposal, assemble a cross-functional working group, and present a remediation path to their peers.
:::

### 2. Genuine Authenticity

**In IT Leadership:** The same person in the board meeting and the sprint retrospective. No code-switching between "executive mode" and "team mode." Your team trusts you because they know who you are, not which version of you will show up today.

**What this looks like:**
- Saying "I don't know" to the CEO with the same comfort as saying it to a junior developer
- Sharing your actual opinion in planning meetings, not the opinion you think the room wants
- Being transparent about constraints and trade-offs rather than presenting false certainty

**Brown's connection:** Authenticity is vulnerability operationalized. Brown's research shows that we can't selectively numb — when we armor up to hide our uncertainty, we also hide our creativity, empathy, and connection.

### 3. Solves Problems Before Anyone Knows They Exist

**In IT Leadership:** The proactive stance. Monitoring before the alert fires. Patching before the exploit. Documenting before the knowledge-holder leaves. Addressing the team conflict before it becomes a resignation.

**What this looks like:**
- Building a capacity plan before the scaling issue hits production
- Having succession conversations before your key architect gives notice
- Identifying the compliance gap before the audit finds it

**Brown's connection:** This requires what Brown calls "choosing courage over comfort." It's uncomfortable to raise a problem that doesn't exist yet — you might look paranoid, wasteful, or pessimistic. But daring leaders surface what they see.

### 4. Infectious Energy

**In IT Leadership:** Not toxic positivity. Not forced enthusiasm. Real, grounded energy that comes from genuine engagement with the work and the people doing it. This is the leader whose presence in a standup changes the temperature of the room.

**What this looks like:**
- Celebrating small wins in the middle of a long migration
- Bringing genuine curiosity to a post-incident review instead of blame
- Showing up energized to the third meeting in a row because the work matters

**Brown's connection:** Brown distinguishes between performed enthusiasm and grounded confidence. Infectious energy flows from leaders who are connected to their purpose and present in their interactions.

## Habits of Mindset

### 5. Thinks Abundantly

**In IT Leadership:** Rejects zero-sum thinking about budget, headcount, credit, and opportunity. An abundant thinker shares credit freely, advocates for peer teams' budget requests, and sees another team's success as validation of the whole technology organization.

**What this looks like:**
- Recommending a peer's team for a high-visibility project because they're the right fit
- Sharing your team's tooling and frameworks with other departments
- Framing budget conversations around value creation, not resource hoarding

**Brown's connection:** Scarcity is one of Brown's core vulnerability triggers. Leaders who operate from scarcity create cultures of hoarding, competition, and political maneuvering.

### 6. Stays Curious

**In IT Leadership:** The leader who asks "why" five times before accepting the surface answer. Who learns the business domain deeply, not just the technology stack. Who treats every incident as a learning opportunity, not a blame assignment.

**What this looks like:**
- Attending conferences outside your domain (UX, product, security) to broaden perspective
- Asking your most junior team member what they see that you don't
- Reading the business case for the project, not just the technical requirements

**Brown's connection:** Brown identifies curiosity as a core element of vulnerability: "Choosing to be curious is choosing to be vulnerable because it requires us to surrender to uncertainty."

### 7. Adapts Quickly

**In IT Leadership:** The leader who can pivot a team's focus without breaking morale. Who can adopt a new methodology without losing the wisdom of the old one. Who treats organizational change as a design problem, not a threat.

**What this looks like:**
- Restructuring your team's approach mid-sprint when customer feedback invalidates assumptions
- Shifting from on-prem to cloud-first without creating a culture of fear
- Absorbing a new team after a reorg and integrating them in weeks, not months

**Brown's connection:** Adaptability requires what Brown calls "learning to rise" — the ability to process the story your brain tells you about change (it's threatening, it means you failed, it means you're not in control) and replace it with a more accurate narrative.

### 8. Owns the Outcome

**In IT Leadership:** No finger-pointing at vendors, legacy systems, business stakeholders, or "the previous team." If you're leading the initiative, the result is yours — good, bad, and ambiguous.

**What this looks like:**
- Saying "The migration failed because I underestimated the data complexity" instead of "The vendor's tool didn't work as advertised"
- Presenting project status with candor, including your own miscalculations
- Holding yourself to the same accountability standards you hold your team to

**Brown's connection:** Brown calls this "accountability" — one of the seven elements of trust. Leaders who externalize blame erode trust systematically, even when their explanations are technically accurate.

## Habits of Execution

### 9. Sees Around Corners

**In IT Leadership:** Strategic foresight. Understanding that today's architectural decision creates tomorrow's technical debt or tomorrow's competitive advantage. Reading industry trends not as news but as signals that change your roadmap.

**What this looks like:**
- Investing in observability before the scaling crisis
- Building API-first when the current system is monolithic because you see the integration future
- Developing your team's cloud-native skills 18 months before the migration mandate arrives

### 10. Goes the Extra Mile

**In IT Leadership:** Not overwork. Not hero culture. The extra mile is doing the work that no one measures but everyone benefits from — documentation, mentoring, cross-team relationships, thoughtful code reviews, well-structured handoffs.

**What this looks like:**
- Writing the architectural decision record that explains not just what you decided but why
- Spending 30 minutes with a struggling team member even though your calendar says you don't have time
- Following up on a stakeholder conversation with a clear summary and next steps without being asked

### 11. Makes Others Feel Special

**In IT Leadership:** The leader who remembers that your daughter had a soccer tournament last weekend. Who notices when someone's energy has changed. Who writes a specific, personal note of appreciation instead of a generic "great job team" email.

**What this looks like:**
- Calling out specific contributions in team meetings by name and with detail
- Advocating for your team members' career advancement in rooms they're not in
- Learning what motivates each individual and connecting their work to that motivation

### 12. Builds Bridges

**In IT Leadership:** The connector. The leader who breaks down silos not by mandate but by relationship. Who knows people across the organization and creates connections that wouldn't exist without them.

**What this looks like:**
- Introducing your security architect to the product designer because their concerns overlap
- Creating cross-functional working groups for problems that don't fit neatly into existing team structures
- Building relationships with non-IT executives so technology decisions incorporate business context
