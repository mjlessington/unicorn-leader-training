# Knowing Your Strengths

## The Unicorn Strengths Assessment for IT Leaders

---

## Beyond Technical Competence

Most IT leaders can articulate their technical strengths with precision: "I'm strong in cloud architecture, data modeling, and system integration." Far fewer can articulate their *leadership* strengths with the same clarity.

Vanderbloemen's framework gives us a language for leadership strengths that goes beyond generic terms like "strategic" or "collaborative." Each of the 12 unicorn traits represents a specific, observable, developable strength.

### Step 2: Identify Your Pattern

Look at where your strengths cluster:

**Heavy in Relationship traits:** You're a natural connector and team builder. Your risk: you may avoid the hard strategic and accountability conversations because relationships feel more comfortable.

**Heavy in Mindset traits:** You're a strong thinker and adapter. Your risk: you may over-index on thinking and under-index on relationship and execution. Great ideas without relational infrastructure don't land.

**Heavy in Execution traits:** You're a doer and deliverer. Your risk: you may drive results at the expense of relationships and may struggle to slow down enough for the mindset work that prevents strategic drift.

**Evenly distributed:** Strong general profile. Your risk: jack of all trades, master of none. Consider whether deepening your top 2-3 would be more impactful than maintaining breadth.

## The Strengths-Vulnerability Intersection

Here is the critical insight this module builds toward: **your greatest strengths are also your greatest vulnerability triggers.**

Examples for IT leaders:

- **Strong in "Sees Around Corners"** → You anticipate problems, which makes you valuable. But when someone doesn't see what you see, your frustration can manifest as condescension or impatience.

- **Strong in "Drives to the Basket"** → You take initiative, which makes you effective. But when your initiative isn't recognized or someone else gets credit, your armor might go up.

- **Strong in "Goes the Extra Mile"** → You deliver beyond expectations. But this can become a form of numbing through overwork, and you may resent others who don't match your effort.

:::tip Vanderbloemen + Brown Integration
Vanderbloemen identifies the habits. Brown identifies the emotional patterns that sustain or undermine those habits. Self-awareness means understanding both: "I'm strong in X, AND here's the emotional pattern that protects and sometimes sabotages that strength."
:::

## The Feedback Loop: External Self-Awareness

Internal assessment is necessary but insufficient. Brown's research shows that leaders who rely solely on self-assessment develop "confident blindness" — high certainty about inaccurate self-perceptions.

### Building External Self-Awareness

**The "What" Question (Eurich):**
Instead of asking "Why did I react that way?" (which tends to produce rationalization), ask "What" questions:
- "What was I feeling when I snapped at the team?"
- "What was I trying to protect?"
- "What would I do differently?"

**The Feedback Request (Brown):**
Ask trusted colleagues a specific, answerable question:
- Not: "How am I doing as a leader?" (too broad, produces platitudes)
- Instead: "When I run our sprint planning, what's one thing I do that works well and one thing that gets in the way?"

**The 360 Pattern (Vanderbloemen):**
Ask 5-7 people who experience your leadership in different contexts (direct reports, peers, your manager, cross-functional partners) to rate you on the 12 unicorn traits. Look for gaps between your self-rating and their ratings.
