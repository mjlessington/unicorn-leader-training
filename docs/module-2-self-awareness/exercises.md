## Exercise 1: Values Identification (20 minutes)

Brown's research shows that daring leaders can identify **two core values** — not ten, not five, two — that are non-negotiable. These are the values you're willing to take a stand for even when it's uncomfortable.

### Step 1: Review this list and circle any that resonate

Accountability, Achievement, Adaptability, Balance, Belonging, Collaboration, Compassion, Courage, Creativity, Curiosity, Dignity, Diversity, Efficiency, Equity, Excellence, Fairness, Faith, Family, Freedom, Fun, Future Generations, Generosity, Growth, Health, Home, Honesty, Humility, Humor, Inclusion, Independence, Innovation, Integrity, Intuition, Joy, Justice, Kindness, Knowledge, Leadership, Learning, Legacy, Love, Loyalty, Making a Difference, Nature, Openness, Optimism, Order, Patience, Patriotism, Peace, Perseverance, Power, Pride, Recognition, Reliability, Resourcefulness, Respect, Responsibility, Risk-taking, Safety, Security, Self-discipline, Self-expression, Service, Simplicity, Spirituality, Stewardship, Success, Teamwork, Thrift, Time, Tradition, Travel, Trust, Truth, Understanding, Uniqueness, Usefulness, Vision, Vulnerability, Wealth, Well-being, Wholeheartedness, Wisdom

### Step 2: Narrow to two

This is hard by design. If everything is a core value, nothing is. Your two core values are the ones that, if removed from your life, would make you feel fundamentally untethered.

**My two core values:**
1. _______________
2. _______________

### Step 3: Operationalize them

For each value, answer:
- **What are 3 behaviors that are aligned with this value?** (Things you do when you're living this value)
- **What are 3 slippery behaviors?** (Things you do when you SAY this value but don't ACT it)
- **What does this value look like in your IT leadership role specifically?**

## Exercise 2: The Rumble Practice (25 minutes)

### For Pairs or Small Groups

Choose one of these IT-specific scenarios (or bring your own real situation):

**Scenario A:** Your best engineer has been underperforming for three months. You've noticed but haven't said anything because they've always been reliable.

**Scenario B:** Your VP wants you to commit to a delivery date that you believe is unrealistic. They've said the date is "non-negotiable."

**Scenario C:** Two of your team leads are in a territorial conflict about ownership of the new microservices platform.

### Rumble Structure (10 minutes per person)

1. **Frame the rumble** (1 min): "The issue I need to address is... The story I'm telling myself is..."
2. **Name your armor** (1 min): "My default protection in this situation would be to..."
3. **Practice the opening** (3 min): Role-play the first two minutes of the conversation. Partner provides real-time feedback.
4. **Debrief** (5 min): What felt hard? Where did the armor want to go up? What did the partner observe?

## Exercise 4: The Mirror and the Window (10 minutes)

Jim Collins' concept adapted for this training: Great leaders look out the **window** to give credit and look in the **mirror** to take responsibility.

Think about the last significant success and failure in your IT leadership:

**Success:**
- Where did you look? (Window: credited the team? Mirror: took the credit?)
- Who specifically deserved recognition that they didn't receive?

**Failure:**
- Where did you look? (Mirror: took responsibility? Window: blamed circumstances or others?)
- What would complete ownership look like?
