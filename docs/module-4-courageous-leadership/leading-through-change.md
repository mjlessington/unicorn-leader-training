# Leading Through Change

---

## The IT Leader's Change Reality

Technology leaders live in a state of constant change. Cloud migrations, organizational restructures, new methodologies, vendor transitions, team changes, strategy pivots — the pace never slows.

The challenge isn't that IT leaders don't know *how* to manage change technically. It's that they underestimate the *human* side of change — the fear, the grief, the uncertainty, and the resistance that accompany every significant transition.

## Brown's Change Framework: The "Day Two" Problem

Brown describes organizational change in three phases:

### Day One: The Launch
Energy is high. The vision is clear. Leadership is excited. The announcement is made. This is where most change management effort goes.

### Day Two: The Messy Middle
Reality hits. The old way is gone and the new way isn't working yet. People are confused, frustrated, and grieving. This is where most change initiatives fail — not because the change was wrong, but because leaders aren't equipped to sit in the discomfort of Day Two.

### Day Three: The New Normal
The change has been integrated. New habits have formed. The organization has adapted. But you can't get to Day Three without going through Day Two.

**The leader's job is to navigate Day Two without armoring up, rushing through it, or pretending it's not hard.**

## The Vanderbloemen Change Playbook

Vanderbloemen's unicorn traits map to specific change leadership behaviors:

### Before the Change
- **Sees Around Corners:** Anticipate how the change will affect each team and individual. Don't wait for resistance — predict it and plan for it.
- **Thinks Abundantly:** Frame the change in terms of opportunity, not just necessity. What becomes possible that wasn't before?
- **Builds Bridges:** Build coalitions before announcing. Identify your change champions and invest in them.

### During the Change
- **Genuine Authenticity:** Be honest about what you know and don't know. Don't perform certainty you don't have.
- **Infectious Energy:** Maintain grounded energy. Not fake enthusiasm — real engagement with the challenges and possibilities.
- **Drives to the Basket:** Take visible action. Don't just announce the change and delegate the implementation. Be in the work.

### In the Messy Middle
- **Owns the Outcome:** When things go wrong (and they will), own it publicly. "I underestimated the impact on your daily workflow, and I'm sorry."
- **Adapts Quickly:** Be willing to adjust the plan without abandoning the vision. Rigidity kills change initiatives faster than resistance does.
- **Stays Curious:** Ask "What are we learning?" not "Why isn't this working?"

### After the Change
- **Goes the Extra Mile:** Don't declare victory and move on. Stay engaged through integration.
- **Makes Others Feel Special:** Recognize the people who made the change work. Specifically, personally, visibly.
- **Solves Problems Proactively:** Identify the secondary effects of the change and address them before they compound.

## The Emotional Journey of Change

IT leaders must understand that every person on their team is on an emotional journey through change:

```
                    Integration
                   /
                  /
Shock → Denial → Frustration → Exploration → Integration
                        \
                         → Disengagement (if not supported)
```

**Your job is not to eliminate negative emotions.** Your job is to create the conditions where people can process them without getting stuck.

### What Each Emotion Needs

| Emotion | What NOT to Do | What to Do |
|---------|---------------|-----------|
| **Shock** | Push for immediate buy-in | Give space. Repeat information. Answer questions. |
| **Denial** | Force acceptance | Be patient. Share evidence. Acknowledge the loss. |
| **Frustration** | Dismiss or minimize feelings | Validate the emotion. Listen without fixing. |
| **Exploration** | Over-direct | Provide resources. Remove barriers. Celebrate experiments. |

## Change Communication for IT Leaders

### The Three Messages People Need

Every communication about change should address:

1. **What is happening and why?** (Facts + context)
2. **What does this mean for me specifically?** (Personal impact)
3. **What support is available?** (Resources + relationship)

### The Vulnerability Cadence

Brown recommends a regular cadence of vulnerable communication during change:

- **Weekly:** What I know, what I don't know, what I'm working to find out
- **Bi-weekly:** What I got wrong, what I've learned, what's changing in the plan
- **Monthly:** How I'm experiencing this change personally (modeling vulnerability)

:::tip[The Anti-Pattern]
The most common IT leadership anti-pattern during change is **over-communicating facts and under-communicating feelings.** Your team doesn't just need the migration timeline. They need to know that you understand this is hard, that their concerns are valid, and that you're committed to supporting them through it.
:::
