# Innovation and Failure

> *"There is no innovation without vulnerability."* — Brene Brown

---

## The Innovation Paradox in IT

Every technology organization wants innovation. Few are willing to accept what innovation actually requires: **the real possibility of failure, publicly, with resources invested and expectations set.**

This creates what we might call the "Innovation Theater" — organizations that celebrate innovation in their values statements while punishing it in their performance reviews. They want the output of innovation (new products, competitive advantage, efficiency gains) without the input (risk, uncertainty, failed experiments, wasted effort).

## What Both Frameworks Say About Failure

### Vanderbloemen: Failure as Data

Vanderbloemen's unicorn traits treat failure not as an event to avoid but as a source of information:

- **Stays Curious:** The unicorn asks "What can we learn?" not "Who's to blame?"
- **Adapts Quickly:** Failed experiments are inputs to adaptation, not evidence of incompetence
- **Owns the Outcome:** Taking ownership of failure removes the shame that prevents learning
- **Drives to the Basket:** If failure is punished, no one will take initiative

### Brown: Failure as Vulnerability

Brown frames the relationship between innovation and failure through the lens of vulnerability:

- Innovation requires creative risk
- Creative risk means uncertain outcomes
- Uncertain outcomes mean emotional exposure
- Emotional exposure is vulnerability
- Therefore: **you cannot have innovation without vulnerability**

This is not a motivational platitude. It's a logical chain. If you remove vulnerability from your organization (through blame, perfectionism, control, or punishment), you remove innovation. Full stop.

## Building a Failure-Positive Culture in IT

### Level 1: Blameless Post-Incident Reviews

The first and most accessible step. When things go wrong in production, the review process focuses on **systems, not individuals.**

**Key principles:**
- Assume every person involved was doing their best with the information they had
- Focus on "What happened?" and "What can we change?" not "Who did this?"
- Publish findings openly — transparency about failure models healthy behavior
- Implement improvements and follow up on their effectiveness

**Unicorn connection:** This directly supports Owns the Outcome (team-level) and Non-judgment (BRAVING).

### Level 2: Sanctioned Experiments

Create explicit channels for controlled risk-taking:

- **Innovation sprints:** Dedicated time for exploring new approaches with clear success criteria and built-in permission to fail
- **Proof of concept budget:** A small, no-approval-needed fund for testing ideas
- **Failure retrospectives:** Regular sessions that celebrate what was learned from experiments that didn't work
- **"Fail fast" criteria:** Define upfront what would tell you to stop, so "failure" is a decision, not a judgment

**Unicorn connection:** Supports Sees Around Corners (strategic experimentation) and Thinks Abundantly (investment in learning).

### Level 3: Vulnerability-Informed Innovation

The deepest level integrates Brown's vulnerability work into the innovation process:

**Normalize the emotional journey of innovation:**
- Excitement → Doubt → Fear → Commitment → Struggle → Breakthrough (or Pivot)
- Every stage involves vulnerability. Leaders who acknowledge this create teams that can sustain the journey.

**Create structures for honest assessment:**
- "Red team" exercises where a group's job is to find weaknesses (without it being personal)
- Regular check-ins that ask "What's the hardest thing about this project right now?" (not just "What's the status?")
- Exit criteria that remove the stigma of stopping a project that isn't working

**Model it yourself:**
- Share your own failed experiments and what you learned
- Publicly acknowledge when you invested in the wrong direction
- Frame your own learning journey as a normal part of leadership

## The Innovation Trust Equation

```
Innovation Capacity = (Technical Skill × Psychological Safety × Resources) ÷ Fear of Failure
```

You can increase innovation capacity by:
1. **Developing technical skills** (necessary but insufficient)
2. **Building psychological safety** (Module 3)
3. **Providing resources** (time, budget, tooling)
4. **Reducing fear of failure** (the highest-leverage intervention)

Most IT organizations focus on #1 and #3. The unicorn leader focuses on #2 and #4.

## Case Study: The Failed Migration

A technology team spent six months building a migration to a new platform. At the five-month mark, they discovered a fundamental architectural flaw that meant the new platform couldn't support a critical business requirement. They had two choices:

**Armored response:** Continue the migration, work around the limitation, and hope it's not a problem. (Avoids vulnerability of admitting the failure.)

**Daring response:** Stop the migration, present the findings to leadership, propose an alternative, and accept that six months of effort didn't produce the intended outcome. (Requires vulnerability, ownership, and courage.)

The armored response is more common. The daring response produces better outcomes.

**What the daring leader does:**

1. Acknowledges the failure to the team first: *"We found something important. This isn't a reflection of anyone's effort or competence — the work you did to get here is exactly what revealed this issue."*

2. Presents to leadership with owned accountability: *"I'm bringing you a difficult finding. We discovered X, which means the approach we've been investing in won't meet our needs. I take responsibility for not identifying this sooner. Here's what I propose instead."*

3. Celebrates the learning: *"Because of the work this team did, we now understand our requirements at a depth we didn't have before. That understanding is an asset, not a waste."*
