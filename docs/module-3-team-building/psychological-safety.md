# Psychological Safety: The Environment Unicorns Need

---

## What Is Psychological Safety?

Amy Edmondson defines psychological safety as *"a shared belief held by members of a team that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking."*

Google's Project Aristotle research confirmed that psychological safety is the #1 predictor of team performance — more than team composition, resources, structure, or individual talent.

For IT leaders, this research has profound implications: **you can hire the most talented engineers in the world, and without psychological safety, they will underperform a mediocre team that has it.**

## Why IT Teams Especially Need It

Technology work involves constant risk:
- **Technical risk:** Every code change, architecture decision, and deployment could break something
- **Intellectual risk:** Asking "dumb" questions, admitting you don't understand the system
- **Interpersonal risk:** Giving feedback, raising concerns, disagreeing with senior engineers
- **Career risk:** Flagging a project as failing, admitting a mistake to leadership

Without psychological safety, people manage these risks by:
- Not asking questions (and making preventable mistakes)
- Not raising concerns (and letting problems escalate)
- Not giving feedback (and letting quality degrade)
- Not admitting mistakes (and making them worse by hiding them)

## The Four Stages of Psychological Safety (Timothy Clark)

### Stage 1: Inclusion Safety
*"I feel safe to be myself on this team."*

**What the leader does:**
- Learns and uses people's preferred names and pronouns
- Creates space for different communication styles (not everyone thrives in real-time debate)
- Ensures remote team members have equal voice in hybrid meetings
- Actively invites perspectives from quieter team members

**Unicorn connection:** Makes Others Feel Special — genuine inclusion, not performative diversity.

### Stage 2: Learner Safety
*"I feel safe to ask questions, make mistakes, and learn."*

**What the leader does:**
- Models curiosity by asking "naive" questions publicly
- Responds to mistakes with "What can we learn?" not "Who's responsible?"
- Creates blameless post-incident reviews that focus on systems, not individuals
- Celebrates learning moments as visibly as successful deliveries

**Unicorn connection:** Stays Curious — leaders who model learning give permission for others to learn.

### Stage 3: Contributor Safety
*"I feel safe to contribute, add value, and apply my skills."*

**What the leader does:**
- Creates clear channels for contribution that don't require navigating hierarchy
- Recognizes contributions specifically and publicly
- Removes barriers that prevent people from doing their best work
- Matches assignments to strengths while stretching capabilities

**Unicorn connection:** Drives to the Basket — people take initiative when they trust that initiative will be received well.

### Stage 4: Challenger Safety
*"I feel safe to challenge the status quo, push back, and disagree."*

**What the leader does:**
- Actively invites dissent: "What am I missing?" "Who sees this differently?"
- Thanks people for challenging ideas, even when it's uncomfortable
- Separates the idea from the person — "I disagree with the approach" not "you're wrong"
- Demonstrates that challenging leadership is safe by not retaliating when it happens

**Unicorn connection:** Genuine Authenticity + Owns the Outcome — leaders who can be challenged without becoming defensive create cultures where truth flows upward.

## Measuring Psychological Safety

Use these seven statements (Edmondson's scale) as a team pulse check. Have each team member rate 1-5:

1. If you make a mistake on this team, it is held against you. *(reverse scored)*
2. Members of this team are able to bring up problems and tough issues.
3. People on this team sometimes reject others for being different. *(reverse scored)*
4. It is safe to take a risk on this team.
5. It is difficult to ask other members of this team for help. *(reverse scored)*
6. No one on this team would deliberately act in a way that undermines my efforts.
7. Working with members of this team, my unique skills and talents are valued and utilized.

**Scoring:** Reverse-score items 1, 3, and 5. Average all seven. Above 4.0 = strong safety. Below 3.0 = urgent attention needed.

:::warning[Common IT Anti-Patterns]
Watch for these psychological safety destroyers common in technology organizations:
- **The "obvious" dismissal:** "That should be obvious" or "As everyone knows..." shuts down learner safety
- **The hero culture:** Rewarding firefighting over prevention penalizes proactive behavior
- **The stack rank:** Forced ranking systems destroy collaboration and information sharing
- **The blame post-mortem:** If anyone gets blamed after an incident, the team will stop reporting incidents
- **The meeting-after-the-meeting:** If real decisions happen in private conversations, people stop participating in public ones
:::

## Brown's Addition: Shame Resilience

Brown's unique contribution to the psychological safety conversation is her research on **shame** — the intensely painful experience of believing we are fundamentally flawed.

In IT culture, shame shows up as:
- The engineer who stops contributing after a code review that felt personal
- The manager who stops proposing ideas after being publicly corrected by their VP
- The team that stops innovating after a failed project was used as a cautionary tale

**Shame resilience** is the ability to recognize shame, move through it constructively, and maintain your sense of worthiness. Leaders build shame resilience in their teams by:

1. **Naming shame** as a normal human experience, not a weakness
2. **Separating behavior from identity** — "The code had a bug" not "you wrote buggy code"
3. **Modeling recovery** — sharing their own experiences with professional shame and how they moved through them
4. **Creating repair rituals** — when shame-inducing events happen (harsh feedback, public mistakes, failed projects), having explicit processes to acknowledge, learn, and move forward
