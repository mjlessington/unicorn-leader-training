# Trust Building: The BRAVING Framework

> *"Trust is built in very small moments."* — Brene Brown

---

## Why BRAVING Matters for IT Leaders

Trust is the word leaders use most and understand least. We say "I trust my team" or "we need to build trust" without specifying what trust actually *is* or how to build it systematically.

Brown's BRAVING inventory breaks trust into seven specific, assessable, developable components. For IT leaders managing complex, interdependent teams, this specificity is transformative — it turns "we have a trust problem" into "we have a reliability problem" or "we have a vault problem," each of which has a different solution.

## The Seven Elements of BRAVING

### B — Boundaries

**Definition:** You respect my boundaries, and when you're not clear about what's okay and not okay, you ask. You're willing to say no.

**In IT Leadership:**
- Respecting after-hours boundaries for non-emergency issues
- Being clear about scope and saying no to scope creep
- Honoring PTO as actual time off, not "working from the beach"
- Setting boundaries around meeting-free focus time

**Trust erodes when:** You agree to boundaries publicly and violate them privately. You say "no meetings on Fridays" but schedule "just this one quick sync."

**Leader action:** Document your team's boundaries. Review them quarterly. Model them yourself.

### R — Reliability

**Definition:** You do what you say you'll do. You're aware of your competencies and limitations so you don't overpromise.

**In IT Leadership:**
- Delivering on committed timelines (or communicating changes early)
- Following through on action items from 1:1s and team meetings
- Being consistent in your behavior (not unpredictably moody)
- Only committing to what you can actually deliver

**Trust erodes when:** You consistently say "I'll get back to you on that" and don't. Your team has learned that your commitments are aspirational, not actual.

**Leader action:** Keep a visible tracking system for your commitments. Under-promise and over-deliver. When you can't deliver, say so early.

### A — Accountability

**Definition:** You own your mistakes, apologize, and make amends. You don't blame others or make excuses.

**In IT Leadership:**
- Taking responsibility for project failures without deflecting to the team
- Apologizing when you make a bad decision, not just when you get caught
- Holding yourself to the same standards you hold others to
- Following through on corrective actions, not just acknowledging the problem

**Trust erodes when:** You hold the team accountable for missing deadlines while excusing your own missed commitments because "leadership priorities shifted."

**Leader action:** Build accountability into team rituals. Start retrospectives with what *you* could have done better. Make self-accountability visible, not performative.

### V — Vault

**Definition:** You keep confidences. You don't share information or experiences that are not yours to share.

**In IT Leadership:**
- Not sharing what an employee told you in a private 1:1 with other managers
- Not gossiping about other teams' problems in your team meetings
- Not using "transparency" as justification for sharing information that was given in confidence
- Keeping salary, performance, and personal information strictly confidential

**Trust erodes when:** Your team discovers you shared something from a private conversation. Even one breach can take months to repair.

**Leader action:** Before sharing any information, ask: "Was this shared with me in confidence? Do I have permission to share it? Is sharing it necessary, or am I seeking social currency?"

:::caution[The Vault Trap]
Many IT leaders violate the vault in a specific way: they share information about one team member with another to "help" them understand each other. "Sarah is going through a tough time, so go easy on her." This feels compassionate but violates Sarah's trust. Instead: "Sarah might need some extra support right now. I'll let her share details if she chooses to."
:::

### I — Integrity

**Definition:** You choose courage over comfort. You practice your values rather than just professing them. You choose what's right over what's fun, fast, or easy.

**In IT Leadership:**
- Pushing back on unrealistic timelines even when it's politically risky
- Reporting security vulnerabilities even when it means rework
- Advocating for your team's wellbeing even when it's inconvenient for stakeholders
- Making architectural decisions based on what's right, not what's expedient

**Trust erodes when:** You tell your team you value work-life balance but expect them to work weekends before a launch. The gap between stated and practiced values is the fastest trust destroyer.

**Leader action:** Identify the top 3 situations where you're tempted to compromise your values. Prepare your response in advance so you're not making integrity decisions under pressure.

### N — Non-Judgment

**Definition:** I can ask for what I need, and you can ask for what you need. We can talk about how we feel without judgment.

**In IT Leadership:**
- Creating space for team members to say "I'm struggling" or "I need help" without it affecting their performance rating
- Not penalizing people for asking questions, even basic ones
- Responding to "I don't know how to do this" with support, not disappointment
- Allowing people to set their own pace of growth

**Trust erodes when:** You say "there are no dumb questions" but your face says otherwise. When someone asks for help and the response carries an unspoken judgment of their competence.

**Leader action:** Monitor your non-verbal responses when people ask for help. Practice genuinely neutral, supportive responses. Remember: judgment destroys the upward flow of information you need as a leader.

### G — Generosity

**Definition:** You extend the most generous interpretation possible to the intentions, words, and actions of others.

**In IT Leadership:**
- Assuming the engineer who missed the deadline was dealing with a blocker, not slacking
- Interpreting the terse Slack message as "busy," not "hostile"
- Reading the stakeholder's repeated scope changes as "under pressure," not "disrespectful"
- Believing your peer had good reasons for their decision, even if you disagree

**Trust erodes when:** You assume the worst about people's motives. In IT, where communication is often text-based and context-poor, ungenerous interpretations spread quickly.

**Leader action:** When you catch yourself making an assumption about someone's intentions, ask: "What's the most generous interpretation of this situation?" Then verify before acting on any assumption.

## Using BRAVING as a Diagnostic Tool

When you sense trust issues on your team, use BRAVING to get specific:

1. **Rate each element 1-5** for your team's current state
2. **Identify the weakest element** — that's where to focus
3. **Design a specific intervention** for that element (not a generic "trust-building exercise")
4. **Reassess after 30 days**

### Example Diagnosis

"My team won't tell me about problems until they're crises."

**BRAVING analysis:**
- Boundaries? Maybe — they may fear taking up your time
- Reliability? Maybe — have you followed through when they raised issues before?
- Accountability? **Likely** — do they fear being blamed?
- Vault? Maybe — will you share their struggles with leadership?
- Non-judgment? **Very likely** — do they fear looking incompetent?
- Generosity? Maybe — do they trust your interpretation of their motives?

**Intervention:** Focus on accountability (model blame-free problem-solving) and non-judgment (respond to problems with curiosity, not frustration).
