# Servant Leadership in IT

---

## Redefining the IT Leader's Role

Robert Greenleaf's servant leadership model asks a fundamental question: *"Do those served grow as persons? Do they, while being served, become healthier, wiser, freer, more autonomous, more likely themselves to become servants?"*

This question reframes the IT leader's purpose. You are not primarily responsible for delivering projects, managing budgets, or implementing technology. Those are outcomes. **Your primary responsibility is to develop the people who do those things.**

## Where Both Frameworks Meet on Service

### Vanderbloemen's Service Traits

Several unicorn traits are inherently service-oriented:
- **Makes Others Feel Special** — Service is personal, not generic
- **Goes the Extra Mile** — Service means doing more than the minimum
- **Builds Bridges** — Service connects people to opportunities they couldn't access alone
- **Thinks Abundantly** — Service comes from a belief that elevating others doesn't diminish you

### Brown's Service Framework

Brown frames service through the lens of empathy and power:
- **Empathy over sympathy:** Servant leaders sit with people in their struggles rather than looking down from a position of comfort
- **Power with, not power over:** Servant leaders share power rather than hoarding it
- **Holding space:** The willingness to be present with someone's pain, confusion, or growth without trying to fix it

## The Five Practices of Servant IT Leadership

### Practice 1: Remove Obstacles Instead of Giving Orders

**Traditional leader:** "We need to increase deployment frequency to daily."

**Servant leader:** "What's preventing us from deploying more frequently? What do you need from me to remove those barriers?"

This is Vanderbloemen's Drives to the Basket applied in service: instead of driving toward *your* goal, you drive toward removing obstacles for *your team's* work.

**Practical applications:**
- Ask each direct report: "What is the single biggest obstacle in your way right now?"
- Spend 30% of your time actively removing obstacles rather than adding new priorities
- Track "obstacles removed" as a personal leadership metric

### Practice 2: Develop People, Not Just Skills

**Traditional leader:** Sends team members to technical training.

**Servant leader:** Invests in the whole person — technical growth, leadership development, career trajectory, and personal wellbeing.

**Practical applications:**
- In every 1:1, dedicate time to career development, not just project status
- Create stretch assignments that develop unicorn traits, not just technical skills
- Advocate for your people's advancement in rooms they're not in (Makes Others Feel Special at the organizational level)

### Practice 3: Share Information Generously

**Traditional leader:** Shares information on a "need to know" basis.

**Servant leader:** Defaults to transparency. Shares context, strategy, and decision-making rationale so the team can make better autonomous decisions.

**Practical applications:**
- Share the "why" behind every significant decision
- Give your team the same business context you have
- When you can't share something, say so honestly: "I can't share the details yet, but here's what I can tell you"

### Practice 4: Measure Success by Team Growth

**Traditional leader:** Measures success by project delivery, uptime, and budget adherence.

**Servant leader:** Measures success by: Are my people growing? Are they taking on bigger challenges? Are they becoming leaders themselves?

**Practical applications:**
- Track how many of your team members have been promoted or taken on expanded roles
- Ask annually: "Is this person more capable than they were a year ago? Is that because of my leadership or in spite of it?"
- Celebrate when team members outgrow your team — that's success, not loss

### Practice 5: Lead from Behind

**Traditional leader:** Is the most visible person in every initiative.

**Servant leader:** Creates space for others to lead, take credit, and grow. Steps forward only when the team needs it.

**Practical applications:**
- Send team members to present to leadership instead of presenting yourself
- When asked a question in a meeting that your team member could answer, redirect: "Alex has been leading this work — Alex, would you share your perspective?"
- Resist the urge to be the smartest person in the room

## The Servant Leadership Paradox

Servant leadership feels counterintuitive in organizations that reward visibility, decisiveness, and authority. IT leaders who serve may feel invisible, undervalued, or passed over.

Brown addresses this directly: *"Leadership is not about being in charge. It's about taking care of those in your charge."*

The paradox resolves over time:
- Servant leaders retain better people (lower recruitment and onboarding costs)
- Servant leaders' teams produce more (because obstacles are removed and people are developed)
- Servant leaders build organizational capacity (because they multiply leaders, not just followers)

The results speak, even if the leader doesn't.
