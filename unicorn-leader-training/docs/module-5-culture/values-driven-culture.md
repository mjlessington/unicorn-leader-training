# Values-Driven Culture in IT Organizations

---

## The Values Gap

Most IT organizations have stated values. Few have operationalized values. The gap between the two is where cynicism grows.

Brown identifies three critical failures in organizational values work:

1. **Too many values:** If you have ten values, you have zero values. No one can internalize and practice ten things simultaneously.
2. **Aspirational vs. practiced:** Values statements that describe who you want to be rather than who you are create cognitive dissonance.
3. **Values without behaviors:** A value without corresponding observable behaviors is a bumper sticker.

## Operationalizing Values: The Brown Method

### Step 1: Identify 2-3 Core Values

Not aspirational values. Not values you think you should have. The values your team actually practices when they're at their best. These might be:

- **Craftsmanship** — We take pride in the quality of what we build
- **Candor** — We tell each other the truth, kindly and directly
- **Curiosity** — We ask questions more than we give answers
- **Ownership** — We own our work end-to-end, successes and failures
- **Service** — We exist to serve our users and our colleagues

### Step 2: Define Observable Behaviors

For each value, define 3-5 specific, observable behaviors that demonstrate it, and 3-5 behaviors that violate it.

**Example: Candor**

| Living the Value | Violating the Value |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| Raising concerns in the meeting, not after it | Agreeing in the meeting and complaining privately |
| Giving specific, actionable feedback within 48 hours | Saving feedback for the annual review |
| Saying "I disagree" with data and respect | Going silent when you disagree |
| Telling stakeholders the honest timeline | Committing to dates you know are unrealistic |
| Asking "What am I missing?" | Presenting conclusions as unchallengeable |

### Step 3: Build Accountability Systems

Values without accountability are suggestions. Build accountability through:

**Hiring:** Ask interview questions that reveal whether candidates practice your specific values.

**Onboarding:** Spend time in the first week explaining what each value means *behaviorally*, with examples and counter-examples.

**Performance conversations:** Include values-aligned behavior as a formal part of performance assessment. Technical excellence without values alignment is not high performance.

**Promotion criteria:** Make it explicit that promotion requires both technical/operational excellence AND values-aligned behavior. The brilliant jerk doesn't get promoted.

**Recognition:** When you publicly recognize someone, name the specific value they demonstrated, not just the outcome they achieved.

## The Unicorn Traits as Cultural Values

Vanderbloemen's 12 traits can serve as the behavioral layer of your cultural values:

| If Your Value Is... | The Unicorn Behaviors That Demonstrate It |
|--------------------|------------------------------------------|
| **Innovation** | Stays Curious, Drives to the Basket, Adapts Quickly |
| **Collaboration** | Builds Bridges, Makes Others Feel Special, Thinks Abundantly |
| **Excellence** | Goes the Extra Mile, Sees Around Corners, Owns the Outcome |
| **Integrity** | Genuine Authenticity, Owns the Outcome, Solves Problems Proactively |
| **Service** | Makes Others Feel Special, Goes the Extra Mile, Builds Bridges |

This mapping gives you a powerful combination: Brown's values framework provides the structure, and Vanderbloemen's traits provide the specific, observable behaviors.

## Cultural Anti-Patterns in IT

Watch for these patterns that undermine values-driven culture:

### The Brilliant Jerk Exception
"They're our best engineer, so we tolerate the behavior."

**Impact:** Signals that technical skill excuses everything. Erodes every value on the wall. Brown calls this the single most damaging cultural compromise a leader can make.

### The Firefighter Reward
Consistently recognizing the person who heroically saves a crisis while ignoring the person who prevented three crises through proactive work.

**Impact:** Trains the organization that crisis response is more valued than crisis prevention. Directly undermines Solves Problems Proactively.

### The Meeting-After-the-Meeting
Real decisions made in private conversations after the formal meeting where "input was gathered."

**Impact:** Destroys candor, inclusion, and trust. People stop contributing because they know it doesn't matter.

### The Accountability Asymmetry
Leaders hold teams accountable for commitments while excusing their own missed commitments.

**Impact:** Erodes Integrity (BRAVING) and Genuine Authenticity. Teams develop cynicism toward any accountability system.

### The Values Poster
Beautiful values statements in the lobby and on the careers page that bear no relationship to daily experience.

**Impact:** Creates active cynicism. Worse than having no values statement at all because it proves leadership either doesn't know or doesn't care about the gap.
