# Brave Conversations for IT Leaders

> *"Clear is kind. Unclear is unkind."* — Brene Brown

---

## The Conversations IT Leaders Avoid

Every IT leader has a list of conversations they know they should have but haven't had yet. Brown calls these "rumbles." Vanderbloemen would call them "driving to the basket." By either name, they are the conversations where growth, trust, and progress are waiting on the other side of discomfort.

### The Top 10 Avoided Conversations in IT Leadership

1. **The performance conversation** — telling someone their work isn't meeting expectations
2. **The scope conversation** — telling stakeholders what's not going to happen
3. **The career conversation** — being honest about someone's readiness (or lack thereof) for promotion
4. **The strategy disagreement** — pushing back on your VP or C-suite when you believe they're wrong
5. **The team conflict** — addressing interpersonal tension instead of hoping it resolves itself
6. **The vendor conversation** — telling a vendor their product isn't working (when there's a relationship to preserve)
7. **The technical debt conversation** — making the business case for work that has no visible customer impact
8. **The restructuring conversation** — explaining organizational changes honestly
9. **The "elephant in the room" conversation** — naming the thing everyone knows but no one says
10. **The self-disclosure conversation** — admitting your own mistake, limitation, or uncertainty

## Brown's Framework for Brave Conversations

### Step 1: Get Clear on Your Intention

Before entering the conversation, answer:
- What is the outcome I want? (Not the outcome I'll settle for — the one I actually want)
- Am I entering this conversation to connect and solve, or to prove and protect?
- What's my role? Am I the person who should be having this conversation?

### Step 2: Prepare Your Emotional Grounding

- Name the emotion you're carrying into this conversation
- Identify your likely armor
- Decide in advance: when the discomfort peaks, what will I do instead of armoring up?

### Step 3: Open with Vulnerability

The first 30 seconds set the tone. Starting with vulnerability (not weakness, not oversharing — genuine emotional honesty) signals that this is a conversation, not a verdict.

**Armored openings:**
- "We need to talk about your performance."
- "I've been getting complaints about your team."
- "Let me give you some feedback."

**Vulnerable openings:**
- "I want to have an honest conversation with you about something I've been sitting on for too long, and that's on me."
- "I care about your growth, and because I care, I need to share something that might be uncomfortable for both of us."
- "I need to talk about something I've noticed, and I want to be straight with you because I respect you."

### Step 4: Stay Curious During the Conversation

Use these phrases to maintain connection:
- "Help me understand..."
- "The story I'm telling myself is... What's your perspective?"
- "I'm curious about what you're experiencing..."
- "Tell me more about that."
- "What do you need from me?"

### Step 5: Land the Plane

Every brave conversation needs a clear landing:
- **Shared understanding:** "Let me make sure we're on the same page..."
- **Agreed next steps:** "Here's what I'm going to do... Here's what I'm asking you to do..."
- **Timeline:** "Let's check in on this in [specific timeframe]"
- **Care:** "I'm invested in this because I'm invested in you."

## IT-Specific Brave Conversation Scripts

### The Performance Conversation

**Context:** Your senior developer has been missing deadlines and their code quality has declined over the past quarter.

**Script framework:**

> "I want to talk with you about something I've noticed over the past few months. I should have raised this sooner, and the fact that I didn't is on me — I was hoping things would self-correct, and that wasn't fair to you.
>
> I've seen a shift in your delivery — timelines are slipping and the code reviews are surfacing more issues than usual. That's not a judgment of your ability — I know what you're capable of because I've seen it. What it tells me is that something has changed, and I want to understand what's going on.
>
> The story I'm telling myself is that something outside of work might be affecting things, or that something about the work itself has changed for you. But I'd rather hear your perspective than guess."

### The Scope Negotiation

**Context:** Your business stakeholder wants to add three major features to a release that's already at capacity.

**Script framework:**

> "I want to be straight with you about what I'm seeing. The three features you've proposed are all valuable — I understand the business case for each of them. And I need to be honest: adding all three to this release means we either compromise quality, miss the date, or burn out the team. None of those outcomes serve us.
>
> What I'd like to do is walk through these together and prioritize. I'll be transparent about the trade-offs for each option so we can make this decision together with eyes wide open."

### The Upward Push-Back

**Context:** Your VP wants to implement a technology your team has evaluated and rejected.

**Script framework:**

> "I want to share a perspective that might differ from where you're heading, and I want to do that because I think it's important, not because I'm being difficult.
>
> We evaluated [technology] and found [specific concerns]. I want to make sure you have that context before we move forward. I could be wrong — I'm open to that — and I also think I owe you my honest assessment. Can I walk you through what we found?"
