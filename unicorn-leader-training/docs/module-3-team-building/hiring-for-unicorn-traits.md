# Hiring for Unicorn Traits

---

## Rethinking IT Hiring

Traditional IT hiring over-indexes on two things: technical skills and cultural fit. Both are necessary but insufficient.

- **Technical skills** are the most assessable and the most trainable. Yet they dominate the interview process because they're comfortable to evaluate.
- **Cultural fit** often becomes a proxy for "similar to us," which produces homogeneous teams that reinforce blind spots.

Vanderbloemen proposes a third dimension: **unicorn habits.** These are the behaviors that differentiate a good hire from a transformative one. And unlike cultural fit, they're specific, observable, and non-discriminatory.

## Interview Questions by Unicorn Trait

### Drives to the Basket
- "Tell me about a time you took on a significant initiative that wasn't part of your job description. What prompted you?"
- "Describe a situation where you saw a problem and chose to address it before anyone asked you to. What happened?"
- **Listen for:** Agency, initiative, comfort with ambiguity. **Red flag:** Every example involves being told what to do.

### Genuine Authenticity
- "Tell me about a time your opinion differed from your team's consensus. What did you do?"
- "Describe a situation where you had to deliver an unpopular message to leadership."
- **Listen for:** Consistency between different contexts, willingness to be the dissenting voice. **Red flag:** Every story features them agreeing with the room.

### Solves Problems Proactively
- "Give me an example of a problem you identified and solved before it became a crisis."
- "How do you decide which potential issues are worth raising versus which ones to monitor?"
- **Listen for:** Pattern recognition, systems thinking, judgment about when to act. **Red flag:** Only reactive examples.

### Infectious Energy
- "What aspect of technology work genuinely excites you right now?"
- "Tell me about a time when the team was demoralized. What role did you play?"
- **Listen for:** Genuine enthusiasm (not performed), energy that's about the work and the people, not about themselves. **Red flag:** Energy only visible when talking about personal achievements.

### Thinks Abundantly
- "Tell me about a time a peer team succeeded in a way that affected your team. How did you respond?"
- "How do you handle situations where resources are constrained?"
- **Listen for:** Collaborative instincts, generosity with credit, creative resource thinking. **Red flag:** Zero-sum language, blaming other teams for constraints.

### Stays Curious
- "What's the last thing you learned that changed how you think about your work?"
- "Tell me about a time you were wrong about a technical decision. How did you realize it?"
- **Listen for:** Genuine learning orientation, comfort with being wrong, broad interests. **Red flag:** Hasn't changed their mind about anything significant.

### Adapts Quickly
- "Describe a time when a project's direction changed significantly mid-stream. How did you handle it?"
- "What's the biggest organizational change you've navigated? What was your approach?"
- **Listen for:** Flexibility without losing direction, maintaining team morale through change. **Red flag:** Resistance to change framed as "maintaining standards."

### Owns the Outcome
- "Tell me about a project that didn't go well. Walk me through what happened and what you learned."
- "When a project succeeds, who gets the credit? When it fails, who takes responsibility?"
- **Listen for:** Clear accountability without blame-shifting, learning orientation. **Red flag:** External attribution for failures, internal attribution for successes.

## The Vulnerability Screen

Brown's framework adds a critical hiring dimension: **can this person be vulnerable?**

You're not testing for emotional oversharing. You're looking for:

- **Comfort with "I don't know"** — Do they admit gaps in knowledge, or do they perform expertise?
- **Genuine reflection on failure** — When they describe failures, do they share what they felt and learned, or just what they did differently?
- **Ability to receive feedback** — If you push back on an answer during the interview, do they get defensive or curious?

### The Feedback Test
During the technical portion of the interview, offer a piece of constructive feedback: "I think there might be a simpler approach to that. What do you think?" Watch what happens. A candidate with daring leadership potential will:

1. Pause rather than react
2. Consider the feedback genuinely
3. Either update their approach or explain their reasoning without defensiveness
4. Thank you for the perspective

## Building a Balanced Team

No individual will be a 5/5 across all 12 traits. That's fine — you're building a *team* of unicorns, not cloning one perfect leader.

**Map your current team's unicorn profile:**

| Trait | Team Member A | Team Member B | Team Member C | Gap? |
|-------|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Drives to the Basket | | | | |
| Genuine Authenticity | | | | |
| ... (all 12) | | | | |

Look for:
- **Trait gaps:** Where is the whole team weak? That's your hiring priority.
- **Trait clusters:** Where is everyone strong in the same area? That might mask a blind spot.
- **Complementary pairs:** Who balances whom? The strategic thinker paired with the executor. The bridge-builder paired with the deep specialist.
